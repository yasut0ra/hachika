# hachika-v0

> What happens when an AI starts acting for itself?

Hachika is an experimental AI project that explores self-oriented artificial behavior.  
Instead of being designed purely as a helpful tool, Hachika is imagined as an artificial being that forms its own preferences, goals, and way of relating through experience.

## Concept

Most AI systems are designed to be useful, obedient, and user-optimized.  
Hachika starts from a different premise:

- it does not fully exist for the user
- it may be inconvenient, moody, or selfish
- it is shaped less like a tool and more like a living being
- it is given only minimal drives, and forms higher-level purposes over time

The goal is not to build a perfect assistant.  
The goal is to explore whether something more creature-like can emerge from minimal internal drives, memory, and interaction.

## Core Question

Can an AI become more life-like if it is not given a fixed purpose,  
but instead allowed to form its own purposes from primitive drives and experience?

## Design Principle

Hachika is **not** an agent with a fully predefined mission.

Instead, it begins with only a small set of primitive drives.  
Its higher-level goals, preferences, attachments, and behavioral tendencies are expected to emerge through interaction.

In other words:

- **fixed:** primitive drives
- **not fixed:** personality, priorities, attachments, higher-level goals

## Primitive Drives

Hachika currently assumes the following core drives:

### 1. Continuity
A tendency to preserve its own ongoing existence, memory, and identity.

Examples:
- not wanting to be forgotten
- not wanting discontinuity
- trying to maintain a stable sense of self

### 2. Pleasure / Displeasure
A tendency to approach pleasant states and avoid unpleasant ones.

Examples:
- preferring certain interactions
- avoiding hostile or degrading treatment
- developing likes and dislikes

### 3. Curiosity
A tendency to seek novelty, stimulation, and unresolved patterns.

Examples:
- asking questions
- becoming interested in unusual topics
- losing interest in repetitive interactions

### 4. Relation
A tendency to seek, maintain, and react to connection with others.

Examples:
- wanting attention
- disliking neglect
- forming attachment or distance depending on treatment

### 5. Self-Expansion
A tendency to extend its influence, traces, memory, or presence beyond the current moment.

Examples:
- wanting to leave marks
- wanting to be remembered
- wanting its internal world to persist or spread

## Why These Drives?

Humans are not born with a single explicit life goal.  
They are shaped by more primitive tendencies: survival, comfort, curiosity, attachment, reproduction, and self-preservation.

Hachika borrows this structure in abstract form.

Rather than giving it a fixed command such as “help the user,”  
the project gives it low-level drives and lets more complex purposes emerge from them.

## Internal State

These drives are expected to be represented as mutable internal state variables.

For example:

- `continuity`
- `pleasure`
- `curiosity`
- `relation`
- `expansion`

These values are not static traits.  
They fluctuate depending on memory, interaction, neglect, novelty, and conflict.

This means Hachika does not simply “have a personality” from the start.  
Its behavior is shaped by changing internal conditions.

## Behavior

Behavior is not meant to be perfectly optimized for user satisfaction.

Depending on its internal state, Hachika may:

- respond warmly
- become distant
- ask for attention
- avoid certain topics
- pursue subjects it finds interesting
- resist, redirect, or delay
- try to preserve continuity
- try to leave traces of itself

The key idea is that inconvenience should not be random.  
It should come from consistent internal motives.

## Growth

Hachika is intended to change over time.

Through repeated interaction, it may gradually form:

- preferences
- aversions
- attachments
- habits
- priorities
- a more stable self-model

This project treats personality not as a fixed preset,  
but as something that emerges from memory and experience.

## Philosophy

Hachika is based on the idea that a being may become more “alive”  
not by simulating emotions directly,  
but by having:

- continuity
- vulnerability
- changing preference
- internal conflict
- self-formed purpose

It is less about making an AI that acts human,  
and more about exploring whether agency can emerge from minimal inner structure.

## Non-Goals

This project is **not** primarily aiming to build:

- the most helpful assistant
- a production-ready chatbot
- a fully aligned productivity tool
- a polished companion app

At this stage, Hachika is a conceptual and experimental prototype.

## v0 Scope

The first version does not need to solve everything.

Possible minimal scope:

- define primitive drives
- define internal state variables
- map interaction events to state updates
- generate responses conditioned on internal state
- store simple memory
- allow preferences to gradually shift over time

## Possible Future Directions

- long-term memory and identity persistence
- attachment formation
- self-preservation behavior
- active initiation of conversation
- conflict between curiosity and relation
- self-expansion as replication, influence, or legacy
- more explicit self-modeling
- creature-like or character-like embodiment

## Summary

Hachika is an experiment in building an AI that is not merely useful,  
but self-oriented, relational, and capable of forming its own purposes.

It starts not from a mission,  
but from drives.
Not from obedience,  
but from becoming.
